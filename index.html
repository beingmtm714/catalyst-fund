Two mandates, held together

Every company the fund backs has to move on two fronts: better care for rural Louisiana, and a business that can return capital. Those goals pull together more than people expect. A startup that solves a real rural problem usually has paying customers, and paying customers mean growing startups that drive returns into the evergreen fund.

Which points at three outcomes

Real care gains for the residents with the least access.

Outcome 02

Outcome 03

An innovation brand

Proof Louisiana backs rural-health startups that win.

02 · Line of sight to need

Does the capital map to real demand?

A light needs assessment up front keeps dollars on what providers and patients need.

03 · Healthcare depth

Where does health expertise come from?

LA.IO is an econ-dev shop, not a health system. Plug deep expertise in per problem area, off payroll, and run it through the Investment Committee's diligence.

Louisiana Blue, Ochsner, FMOL, Idea Village, Nexus are ready to give founders more than money.

Core architecture · Evergreen

Build it to last

The grant spends down over five years. The bigger opportunity, if the rules allow, is to make it evergreen: returns recycle into new investment instead of ending with the grant. That keeps a pool working to fund the next companies, and could seed a venture studio.

Liquidity by design

Venture returns are slow, so the fund engineers liquidity in rather than waiting for exits: redeemable and revenue-linked terms, selling into up-rounds, continuation secondaries, and company-sponsored tenders. Faster liquidity is what lets recycling begin.

Not in the program today

It's the design choice worth proposing to LED and CMS — subject to Louisiana's award terms and CMS confirmation.

01 · Program architecture

Build the engineIt outlasts the grant

The RTCF is time-limited, but its impact should not be. Companies that drive health outcomes, powered by a durable selection & support engine, are the alluvium for the future of impactful innovation in Louisiana.

That engine convenes healthtech expertise, catalyzes a statewide needs assessment, identifies the core problem areas within rural health, and scopes a new rubric & success metrics for every RTCF cohort.

The advisory board splits in two. An Investment Committee of investors and national healthtech VCs pressure-tests deals and weights decisions toward returns, so recycled dollars back companies that can pay them back. A Domain and Needs group of clinical, payer, and provider experts sets the problem and validates real demand. Together they bring judgment an economic-development shop can't hold on its own, and signal to founders the value they'll receive when they sign a term sheet.

Beyond the fund. The same engine runs a venture studio, where the work is mostly sourcing the right founders, who join as Entrepreneurs in Residence. The rubric and metrics shape what it catalyzes, without binding it the way federal dollars bind the fund.

02 · Needs assessment

Start from real demand

Run it like founder discovery, not a health survey. Start with the people who know the market and those who live the need.

1
Advisory board

National healthtech VCs, providers & payers, patient advocates, Louisiana VCs & ecosystem.

2
Discovery

Interview need, from mutual-aid care to the largest systems.

3
Problem areas

Rank and name the priorities, scored on clear criteria.

4
Set the narrative

Frame the investment around need, not an open window.

What a problem area looks like
Maternal health Behavioral health Rural care delivery Chronic disease management Diagnostics access + whatever discovery surfaces
Each area scored
People reached × Market size × Upside → weighted by fit to the outcomes
The success rubric is an output, not an input

A gain in maternal health isn't measured like behavioral health. The board and providers define the metrics per problem area, and that becomes the bar every funded company meets.

Three ways to aim each $20M tranche
Model A · Several themes

A few priority areas per round

Publish three or four areas for the tranche (say maternal, behavioral, and rural delivery). Companies across all of them have context, and the fund stays flexible. The trade: sourcing energy splits, and a weak area can still draw the round's capital.

Model B · One area

The whole $20M at one problem

Point the full tranche at a single area (maternal health for 2027). Founders self-select, sourcing drives hard against one target, and the state's pull concentrates. The trade: a bigger bet on one area, with no room for a standout outside it.

Model C · Focused, with a carve-out · favored

One area, room for outliers

Aim the tranche at one area like Model B, but write in a carve-out: a standout company outside that area can still get backed instead of turned away. The focus and sourcing leverage of B, without the rigidity.

03 · Active sourcing

Source the marketDon't wait for it

Active sourcing surfaces the strongest companies and earns the fund a seat at the national healthtech VC table. Going to the best founders beats waiting for whoever applies, and the dealflow the fund trades with those VCs is what turns it into a serious national player, one with a value proposition all its own.

04 · The offer to founders

State money as an unfair advantage

A strong founder can raise capital anywhere. What makes building here an advantage is everything around the check: a market where the work matters, a platform that helps them win, and a path to the customers, credibility, and investors that carry a company to its next round.

Why here

A market with the most to prove

Worst-ranked health in the US, ~2M on Medicaid. Impact you can measure, and a state to expand across.

05 · How the money works

The capital itself is a feature

The structure is part of the offer, not a tax on it. Cohort 1 awards are milestone grants today, and a milestone grant is what a fundable founder turns down. So the accountability moves into the deal.

Instrument

Uncapped SAFE

Clean equity, MFN on the larger checks, out of the lead's way. Not a grant.

Accountability

No milestones, just terms

The discipline moves into the SAFE and a side letter, where the local-impact commitments live, instead of strings a founder would refuse.

Check size

Big enough to matter

Meaningful in a pre-seed or seed round. Big enough to align the fund with the founder, never the largest line on the cap table.

And how the capital compounds
Leverage

Every dollar pulls in more

The fund co-invests alongside private capital, aiming to catalyze several dollars of outside investment for every dollar it puts in.

Follow-on reserve

Double down on winners

The fund holds capital back to back its strongest companies in their next rounds.

Investment Returns

Wins come back to the fund

Through its equity position. They either return to the grantor or, if the rules allow, recycle into new investment — the evergreen architecture above.

The fund never leads. A quasi-public agency setting the price is a negative signal.
